# Voice And Rhythm

Use this reference when calibrating the sound of the article: directness, paragraph rhythm, humor, reversal, and emotional intensity.

## Corpus Rhythm Signals

Across the extracted article segments:

- Median body length was roughly 2700 characters.
- Median non-space line length was roughly 22 Chinese characters.
- About 47 percent of non-empty lines were 20 characters or shorter.
- Rhetorical questions and exclamation marks were frequent, but declarative sentences still carried most of the article.
- Numbered mini-sections appeared often when the piece stacked stories or lessons.

Use these signals to shape rhythm, not to mechanically imitate punctuation counts.

## Voice Principles

- Talk to one reader, not a demographic report.
- Keep the surface conversational and the underlying logic firm.
- Let jokes release pressure, not replace argument.
- Use self-mockery before judging others when the article risks sounding superior.
- Turn abstract values into ordinary scenes: a bill, a chat reply, a family dinner, a commute, a bag, a deadline.
- Prefer clean, sharp language over ornate lyricism.

## Useful Moves

### Direct Address

Use `你` when the article should feel like a private confrontation. Use `我们` when the piece needs complicity or collective recognition. Use `我` when the argument needs vulnerability or comic self-exposure.

### Reversal Pair

Use a contrast pair to sharpen the point:

- `不是 A，而是 B`
- `你以为 A，其实 B`
- `表面上是 A，真正发生的是 B`
- `最可怕的不是 A，是 B`

Do not stack too many reversal pairs in a row. One strong turn per section is usually enough.

### Emotional Naming

After a scene, name what the reader may be feeling:

- shame
- anger
- exhaustion
- being used
- being invisible
- fear of losing
- wanting money without wanting to be vulgar
- wanting love without wanting to kneel

Use plain words. The emotional naming should feel like recognition, not therapy jargon.

### Comic Compression

Use jokes to make a social absurdity visible:

- Exaggerate the hidden demand.
- Compare an emotional transaction to an everyday business transaction.
- Reduce a grand moral excuse to a small selfish benefit.

Avoid jokes that depend on protected traits, body shaming, or cruelty toward vulnerable people.

### Punchline Paragraph

Put a single sentence in its own paragraph when it does one of these jobs:

- flips the argument
- delivers a boundary
- names the hypocrisy
- turns pain into laughter
- gives the reader permission

## Intensity Ladder

Choose an intensity level before drafting.

1. Gentle: warm, reflective, still pointed.
2. Crisp: direct but not aggressive.
3. Sharp: high-contrast, frequent second person, clear judgment.
4. Combative: confrontational, uses rhetorical questions and hard boundaries.
5. Nuclear: only use when the user explicitly wants a polemic and the target is an idea, behavior, or public argument, not a protected group or private person.

## Punctuation Discipline

- Use question marks for real pressure, not decoration.
- Use exclamation marks after a strong comic or emotional turn, but avoid shouting across whole paragraphs.
- Use ellipses for hesitation, embarrassment, or comic timing.
- Do not rely on punctuation to create intensity. The claim must already be strong.

## Revision Pass

After drafting, revise once for rhythm:

1. Cut abstract setup before the first concrete scene.
2. Split paragraphs longer than four lines.
3. Move the strongest verdict closer to the end of its section.
4. Replace generic adjectives with visible behaviors.
5. Remove repeated insults and keep the most precise judgment.
